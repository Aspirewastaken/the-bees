#### Step 1: Start with what we know about intelligence in nature.

**New Flaw: Assumption of Evolutionary Optimality.** Evolution optimizes for reproductive fitness, not intelligence per se. Brains evolved not for "intelligence" but for survival in ancestral environments (e.g., foraging, social cooperation). Many organisms survive without high intelligence (e.g., sponges, insects). The brain's system architecture may reflect path-dependent constraints (e.g., incremental evolution from simpler structures) rather than optimality. AI development is not bound by evolution's constraints—compute is abundant, and we can design from scratch. This analogy assumes evolution's "optimization time" proves system superiority, but evolution often produces suboptimal solutions (e.g., the human appendix, inefficient bipedalism). A single large model could be the optimal path in a compute-rich world, without the trade-offs that forced biological modularity.
